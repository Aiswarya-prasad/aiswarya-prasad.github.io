A few weeks after my PhD defense, I find myself in a reflective mood. The adrenaline has worn off and the post-defense high — that surreal lightness of having handed in your thesis and stepped out of the exam room into a room full of people you love — has settled into something quieter and more durable. This post is my attempt to capture some of that reflection: why I did a PhD, how I ended up in Switzerland, and what I would tell someone starting out today. I also wanted to document how we celebrated, because the party was, if I do say so myself, a small triumph of multicultural hospitality.

## Why PhD

### At the time

My motivation at the time was genuinely simple: I wanted to go deep. I was interested in the microbiome — the communities of microorganisms that live in and on us — and I wanted the training to do rigorous, original research rather than just apply existing methods. A PhD seemed like the right vehicle for that: years of focused work on a single set of questions, with the freedom to follow an idea wherever it led.

There was also the pull of going abroad. I wanted to widen my exposure — intellectually, culturally, personally. A PhD at a research-intensive European institution felt like a rare chance to do all of that at once: gain deep technical expertise, develop a research philosophy, and live somewhere genuinely different from where I had grown up.

### In retrospect

The PhD delivered on those promises, though not always in the ways I expected. The technical training was real and valuable, but what has stayed with me more is the philosophy of science it forced me to develop. You spend so long inside a single question that you start to interrogate not just the question itself but the assumptions behind it — why this question, why this model organism, why this methodology. That kind of reflexive thinking about how knowledge is constructed is something I did not anticipate gaining, and I find it genuinely useful.

I will also share what might be a hot take: a PhD does not make you industry-ready in any direct sense. Most PhD projects are individually driven, with loose timelines, a small number of stakeholders who largely share your background and motivations, and very few of the constraints that define work in a company or policy environment. That is not a criticism — it is a feature, not a bug, of what a PhD is meant to be. But if you are going into one expecting it to prepare you for a specific career path, I would temper that expectation. What it does give you is a platform from which you can make yourself ready for many things — if you are lucky with your lab, your location, and how proactively you use the time.

## Why Switzerland

### At the time

Honestly? The lab I wanted to work in happened to be there. I had identified a PI whose research I found genuinely exciting, and the fact that they were in Switzerland was secondary to the science.

### In retrospect

Switzerland turned out to be an excellent place to do a PhD beyond the science. PhD stipends in Switzerland are among the highest in the world in absolute terms, and the cost of living — while high — is manageable on a researcher's salary in a way that is not true in many cities. The country has a remarkably active innovation and startup ecosystem, particularly in the life sciences, which made it easier to see paths beyond academia. Quality of life is high, the country is beautiful, and a genuine emphasis on work-life balance in Swiss culture means that boundaries around evenings and weekends are more respected than in many research environments I have heard about elsewhere.

That said, there are real challenges. The language barrier is not insurmountable, but it is real — French, German, or Italian will serve you in daily life in ways that English alone will not. And for people classified as "third-country nationals" (non-EU/EFTA citizens), pivoting out of academia into the Swiss job market is genuinely difficult. Work permits are tied to your research position, and transitioning into industry or even other academic roles requires navigating a system that is not designed with you in mind. I did not fully appreciate how much this would matter until I was in it.

## How did I get there

### At the time

I contacted my PI cold — an unsolicited email — and I followed up three times before getting a response. I want to be honest about this because I think the persistence mattered. The conversation that followed was warm and genuinely exciting; it was clear we were aligned on where the field was going and what kind of questions were worth asking.

I was supported by an FBM fellowship, which covers PhD students in the Faculty of Biology and Medicine. What I think helped my application was a combination of factors: I had wet lab experience from previous projects, and I had been actively building skills in bioinformatics at a time when the lab was expanding into metagenomics and NGS-based approaches. I was not an expert — I had taught myself the basics of R through an ecology course, picked up bash from curious friends who had nudged me into using Linux, and challenged myself to learn Snakemake for my master's thesis. None of it was deep expertise at the time, but together it signaled genuine interest in a direction the lab was moving toward, not just in what the lab was already doing.

### In retrospect

There is an element of right place, right time to all of this that I want to acknowledge. The funding environment in Switzerland was favorable, the specific skill set I was building overlapped with an emerging need in the field, and the interview went well. A lot of it was luck — or more precisely, luck intersecting with preparation.

What I can say with more confidence is that demonstrating interest in a forward-looking area — rather than just mirroring the lab's current work — seemed to matter. If you are applying to PhD positions, I would think carefully not just about whether your background fits what the lab is doing now, but about where the field is going and whether you can speak to that.

The funding environment is more challenging now than it was when I started. I would encourage anyone going through this process to apply broadly, reach out early, and not interpret silence as rejection — persistence, where appropriate, is reasonable.

## Celebrating the defense

I had been thinking about the celebration for longer than I would like to admit. One of the things I find most delightful about having spent several years in a multicultural research environment is that "celebration food" does not have to mean any one thing. So we went for a spread that reflected the people in the room.

We started with a classic Swiss apero — good bread, cheese, charcuterie — and then moved into South Indian snacks that I had been looking forward to making for weeks: sundal, idly with a few different podis, and some homemade chutneys. We also had German mini sausages for the colleagues who had become a kind of chosen family over the years, and a dessert table with cakes. It was a lot of food, and it was exactly right.

## Writing the thesis

The thesis writing period is one of the more disorienting parts of the PhD — you are doing intensely solitary work on something that feels simultaneously very high-stakes and very finished. You have already done most of the science; you are now just trying to represent it faithfully and compellingly. Here is what helped me:

**Read broadly at first, then selectively.** I read a lot of theses and papers at the start — more broadly than felt necessary — and then returned repeatedly to the handful that I found genuinely inspiring. Having a few reference points that you admire, stylistically or scientifically, is worth more than a large pile of things you have skimmed.

**Collect theses you respect.** Not just papers — actual PhD theses. They model the genre you are writing in, at the scale you are writing at. Find ones from researchers whose work you admire and read them closely.

**Make a very detailed task breakdown.** Not "write Chapter 3" but "compile figures for Chapter 3," "replot the colonization boxplots," "draft the results text for the priority effects section." The more granular the list, the more tractable the work feels and the more clearly you can see your progress.

**Build a small, trusted peer group.** Share your detailed task list with them. Compare notes on where you are stuck. The accountability and solidarity of other people who are in the same phase — ideally people with different advisors and projects, so there is no competitive dynamic — is genuinely valuable.

**Treat the draft as a living document for your PI.** Busy advisors can engage more easily with a document that is already moving than with a blank page or a question. Get something on the page and update it regularly; it makes the feedback loop much smoother.

**Remember that you have been preparing to write this for two years.** The thesis is not something you write in two months — it is the crystallization of two or more years of reading, thinking, and doing. The months of active writing are just the last phase. Try to carry that perspective with you when the writing feels slow.

**Resist the urge to open new directions.** When you are deep in writing, ideas for new experiments or new framings will surface. Almost all of them should be noted and set aside. Adding a new direction to an almost-finished section is not a sign of intellectual vitality; it is a sign of avoidance. Trust the work you have done.

**If you are visually inclined, draw something.** I made some illustrations that never ended up in the thesis, but making them helped me think and gave me a small pleasure during an otherwise gray period.

**Do Zoom co-working with a trusted peer or friend.** Working in parallel on a video call — not talking, just present — is surprisingly effective for sustaining focus through long writing sessions. Pick someone whose company you enjoy even in silence.

### A sample timeline

One thing I found helpful was having both a coarse multi-week timeline and a very granular week-level breakdown. Here is what mine looked like for the final stretch:

**Week 1 (21–25 Apr):** Complete Chapter 3 draft and discussion. Clean up other sections. Share draft for feedback. Begin outlining General Introduction by section.

**Week 2 (28 Apr – 2 May):** Write General Introduction paragraphs as time permits. Trip with a few activity-free days built in. Lab celebration on the 2nd.

**Week 3 (5–9 May):** Write up the General Introduction in full. Write Chapter 3 and Chapter 2 summaries. Draft abstracts and share for feedback.

**Week 4 (12–16 May):** Complete General Discussion. Incorporate feedback on General Introduction and abstracts. Write acknowledgements.

**Week 5 (19–22 May):** Prepare full thesis draft including CV and any required additional sections. Polish and proofread. Add embellishments — figures, cover page. Combine manuscript PDFs and supplementary material.

**22 May:** Send thesis draft to committee and doctoral school.

The granular version looked something like this for the figures in one chapter:

- Compile all figures and tables; polish code and separate figure-generation scripts from analysis code
- Fig. 1 (experimental design): mostly done — minor polishing
- Fig. 2 (PCoA plots): replot and refine color scheme
- Fig. 3 (colonization boxplots): replot without shape encoding for community combinations; include more detailed version in supplementary
- Fig. 4 (priority effects per strain): show medians with bootstrapped confidence intervals; full bootstrap figure in supplementary
- Table 1: GLMM results for abundance ~ arrival order + random effect
- Fig. 5 (dropout treatments): streamline boxplots, add statistics

It is tedious to make a list this detailed. It is also, I found, completely worth it.
